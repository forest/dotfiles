# The Elements of Style — Essential Summary

*William Strunk Jr. & E. B. White, 4th Edition*

---

## I. Elementary Rules of Usage

1. **Form the possessive singular by adding 's** — regardless of the final consonant. *Charles's friend, Burns's poems.* Exceptions: ancient proper names (*Jesus', Moses' Laws*) and phrases like *for conscience' sake.*

2. **Use a serial comma** — in a series of three or more terms, place a comma after each term except the last. *Red, white, and blue.*

3. **Enclose parenthetic expressions between commas** — if you use one comma, you must use both. Nonrestrictive clauses (those adding information rather than defining) are parenthetic. *The audience, which had been indifferent, grew interested.*

4. **Place a comma before a conjunction introducing an independent clause** — *The situation is perilous, but there is still one chance of escape.*

5. **Do not join independent clauses with a comma** — use a semicolon, a period, or a conjunction. *Mary Shelley's works are entertaining; they are full of engaging ideas.*

6. **Do not break sentences in two** — do not use a period where a comma belongs. Fragments are permissible only for deliberate emphasis.

7. **Use a colon after an independent clause to introduce a list, appositive, or quotation** — the colon must follow a complete clause, never a verb or preposition.

8. **Use a dash to set off an abrupt break or a long appositive** — stronger than a comma, less formal than a colon. Use sparingly; a more common mark is usually sufficient.

9. **The number of the subject determines the number of the verb** — words between subject and verb do not change this. *Everybody thinks he has a unique sense of humor.*

10. **Use the proper case of pronoun** — *who* vs. *whom*, *I* vs. *me*. When in doubt, substitute *he/him* to test: if *he* works, use *who*; if *him* works, use *whom.*

11. **A participial phrase at the beginning of a sentence must refer to the grammatical subject** — *Walking slowly down the road, he saw a woman* (not *he saw a woman walking*).

## II. Elementary Principles of Composition

1. **Choose a suitable design and hold to it** — foresee the shape of what is to come and pursue that shape.

2. **Make the paragraph the unit of composition** — each paragraph develops one topic. Begin with a sentence that signals the topic or aids the transition.

3. **Use the active voice** — *I shall always remember my first visit to Boston* is stronger than the passive alternative. The passive is not forbidden, but habitual use of the active makes for forcible writing.

4. **Put statements in positive form** — make definite assertions. Avoid tame, hesitating, noncommittal language. Say what *is*, not what *is not*. *He usually came late* beats *He was not very often on time.*

5. **Use definite, specific, concrete language** — prefer the particular to the general. *It rained every day for a week* beats *A period of unfavorable weather set in.*

6. **Omit needless words** — every word should tell. Cut: *the fact that, in order to, due to the fact that, who is, which was, there is/are, used for fuel purposes.* Vigorous writing is concise.

7. **Avoid a succession of loose sentences** — a string of clauses joined by *and* or *but* becomes monotonous. Vary with simple sentences, periodic sentences, or semicolons.

8. **Express coordinate ideas in similar form** — parallel construction signals parallel content. *Not charity, but simple justice.*

9. **Keep related words together** — the subject and its verb, the verb and its object, should not be separated by phrases that could move elsewhere.

10. **In summaries, keep to one tense** — use the present tense for summaries of drama, fiction, and poetry. Do not shift tenses without reason.

11. **Place the emphatic words of a sentence at the end** — the most prominent position is the end; the second most prominent is the beginning. Bury weak words in the middle.

## III. A Few Matters of Form

- **Exclamation marks** — reserve them for true exclamations or commands, not to dress up ordinary statements.
- **Hyphen** — use to join two or more words forming a compound adjective before a noun (*leisure-class pursuits*). When in doubt, consult a dictionary.
- **Quotations** — formal quotations are introduced by a colon; quotations in apposition or as direct objects are preceded by a comma. The comma and period always fall *inside* the closing quotation mark.
- **That vs. Which** — *that* introduces restrictive (defining) clauses with no comma; *which* introduces nonrestrictive (adding) clauses set off by commas. *The lawn mower that is broken is in the garage* (which one). *The lawn mower, which is broken, is in the garage* (only one mower).
- **Numerals** — write dates and serial numbers as figures. Spell them out only in dialogue.

## IV. Words & Expressions to Watch

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| *utilize* | *use* |
| *due to the fact that* | *because* |
| *in terms of* | rephrase directly |
| *hopefully* (as sentence adverb) | *I hope* / *we hope* |
| *irregardless* | *regardless* |
| *impact* (as verb) | *affect*, *change* |
| *finalize* | *complete*, *finish* |
| *interesting*, *meaningful* | say exactly what you mean |
| *very*, *rather*, *pretty*, *little* (as qualifiers) | cut them or use a stronger word |
| *He is a man who…* | *He…* |
| *due to* (adverbial) | *because of*, *owing to* |
| *different than* | *different from* |
| *less* (for countable things) | *fewer* |
| *disinterested* | only means *impartial*, not *uninterested* |

## V. An Approach to Style — The Reminders

1. **Place yourself in the background** — draw attention to the writing, not to yourself.
2. **Write in a way that comes naturally** — use words that come readily to hand, but revise ruthlessly.
3. **Work from a suitable design** — know the shape of what you are writing before you begin.
4. **Write with nouns and verbs** — adjectives and adverbs are supporting players; nouns and verbs carry the load.
5. **Revise and rewrite** — a first draft is a starting point, not a finished product.
6. **Do not overwrite** — rich, ornate prose is hard to digest. Cut the excess.
7. **Do not overstate** — one superlative carelessly placed destroys the reader's trust.
8. **Avoid qualifiers** — *rather, very, little, pretty* are leeches. Cut them.
9. **Do not explain too much** — let dialogue and action speak. Resist loading attributives with adverbs (*he said consolingly*).
10. **Avoid fancy words** — prefer the short Anglo-Saxon word over the long Latin one. *Use*, not *utilize*. *Show*, not *demonstrate*.
11. **Be clear** — when a sentence becomes hopelessly tangled, start fresh. Break it into two. Clarity is the nearest thing writing has to a virtue that substitutes for merit.
12. **Revise and rewrite** — few writers produce what they are after on the first try.
13. **Prefer the standard to the offbeat** — enter the stream of English quietly. Established usage gives the beginner the best chance of doing well.

*"Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences — for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."*
— William Strunk Jr.
